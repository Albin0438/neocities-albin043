Why I Stopped Distro-Hopping and Settled on CachyOS

Apr 24, 2026 · 4 min read

If you've used Linux for a while, you've probably gone through it—the endless cycle of distro-hopping. Trying something new, customizing everything, breaking it, fixing it, and then moving on again.

I’ve tried a lot of distributions over time. Ubuntu, Linux Mint, and several Arch-based systems. Each one had something interesting, but none of them felt like something I wanted to stick with long-term.

The problem wasn’t the distributions themselves—it was me.

I was always chasing the “perfect setup.” The fastest system, the cleanest UI, the most minimal environment. But every time I got close, I would find something small to complain about and start over again.

Eventually, I landed on CachyOS.

At first, it was just another experiment. But something about it felt different. It was fast, responsive, and came with sensible defaults that didn’t get in my way.

More importantly, I stopped thinking about my system all the time.

That’s when I realized something: the best system isn’t the one you tweak endlessly—it’s the one you forget about.

CachyOS gave me that. I could focus on actually using my computer instead of constantly rebuilding it.

It’s not perfect. No system is. But it’s stable enough, fast enough, and flexible enough for what I need.

In the end, distro-hopping wasn’t about finding the perfect OS—it was about avoiding settling down. Once I stopped chasing perfection, I finally found something that worked.
