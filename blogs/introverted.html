On Being Introverted in a Noisy World

Feb 22, 2026 · 4 min read

For a long time, I thought being introverted was a disadvantage. Not because of what it actually is—but because of what the modern world seems to reward.

Loud voices, constant presence, endless communication. It creates the impression that if you're not always visible, you're falling behind.

But that idea doesn’t really hold up when you look at history.

Many thinkers, scientists, and creators did their best work in solitude. Not because they avoided people—but because deep work requires distance from noise.

The problem today isn’t introversion. It’s distraction.

We live in an environment where silence is rare. Phones, notifications, short-form content—it constantly pulls attention away from sustained thinking. In that kind of world, being comfortable alone is not a weakness. It’s an advantage.

I used to overthink a lot—mostly about not being “outgoing enough.” But over time, I started noticing something: when I was alone and focused, I actually made progress.

Learning, building, writing—none of that required being loud. It required consistency and attention.

That doesn’t mean isolation is the answer. But it does mean that constantly trying to become someone else is unnecessary.

Maybe the goal isn’t to become extroverted. Maybe it’s to use what already comes naturally—and build from there.
